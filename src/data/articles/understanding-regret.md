# Understanding Regret and How to Process It

## Why the Past Keeps Replaying, and What to Do With It

If there is something playing on a loop in your mind right now, some version of "I should have" or "if only I had," I want to start here. You are not weak for not being able to shut it off. You are not broken for still caring about something that happened a long time ago. The mind does not work that way, and it was never meant to.

I write to a particular kind of reader. The woman who has woken up at three in the morning and found her mind running the same loop of "I should have" until the sky started turning gray. Maybe you have been there once or twice. Maybe you have lived there for a long time, and what you are carrying cannot be fixed, and that is part of why it will not leave you alone.

Whatever you are carrying, the fact that you still care about what happened is itself a sign of something good in you. We will walk through why regret sticks, what to do with it when it does, and what Scripture offers people who have been turning the same thing over for a long time. None of this is meant to make you feel worse about what you already feel bad about.

### Why It Will Not Leave You Alone

The research on regret has clarified one thing that I think most of us were never told. Regret does not stick because we are weak. It sticks because of something psychologists call the opportunity principle. Regret intensifies in places where the door still feels open, even slightly. That is why a missed conversation can ache more than a closed business. It is why the words we did not say keep replaying long after the conversation ended. The mind keeps generating alternatives because some part of it still believes a different outcome might be possible.

Researchers have also found that regrets about things we did not do tend to linger longer than regrets about things we did. When we acted and it went wrong, the situation closes itself. When we did not act, the mind keeps running the simulations, because nothing ever closed the loop.

So if you are stuck in a pattern of "I should have," you are not weak. You are encountering one of the most common and most studied emotional patterns in human life. The work is not to make it stop. The work is to learn what it is for.

### The Cost of Trying to Outrun It

The first instinct with painful emotion is to push it away. We stay busy enough that it cannot catch up, or we reframe it quickly before it has a chance to land. None of that works for long, and most of us already know it.

Suppression does not resolve regret. It drives it underground, where it shows up later as rumination, anxiety, low-grade depression, or a vague sense of being stuck without knowing why. Some research has linked chronic unprocessed regret to elevated stress markers and physical complaints, which is one more way the body keeps trying to tell us what the mind keeps refusing to hear.

The cost of avoiding regret is not freedom from regret. It is regret without the benefit of reflection.

### A Way Through

A workable process for regret, drawn from both clinical research and pastoral counseling, looks something like this. None of it requires special training. All of it requires honesty.

Name what the feeling actually is. Not "I feel bad," which is vague enough that the mind can keep running. Something more specific, like "I regret that I did not call her before she died." Vagueness is what keeps regret circling. Specificity gives it somewhere to land.

Identify what kind of regret you are dealing with. Is it about something you did, something you failed to do, or something you are afraid you will choose wrongly in the future? Each one calls for a different response, and treating them all the same is part of why processing regret often feels impossible.

Separate the changeable from the closed. Some regrets can still be addressed through repair, or through a different choice next time. Others belong to a season that is genuinely over, and we will sit with those in a moment. The mind tends to treat all regret as if it were still actionable, which is part of why closed regrets feel especially exhausting. They cannot be acted on, so they have nowhere to go except in circles.

Move from rumination to reflection. Rumination asks the same question over and over without ever accepting an answer. Reflection asks what the regret is trying to teach you. The wording matters more than it sounds. One keeps you stuck. The other moves you forward.

Practice self-compassion, which is not the same as letting yourself off the hook. Research consistently identifies harsh self-talk as one of the main mediators that keeps regret alive in the body. Speaking to yourself the way you would speak to a friend in the same situation is not soft. It is one of the more clinically effective things you can do.

When repair is possible, take it. Some regrets are not asking to be released. They are asking to be addressed. An honest apology, or a real attempt to make something right that you can still make right, will do more for the loop in your head than any amount of journaling about it.

### When Repair Is Not Possible

There is a particular kind of regret I want to address separately, because the rest of what is written about regret does not always serve the person carrying it. This is the regret over something that cannot be fixed. The person is gone, or the chance has long since passed, and the choice you wish you could go back and unmake belongs to a year you cannot return to. You can apologize to the wall all you want. The wall cannot accept it.

If this is what you are carrying, please hear me. The fact that there is no repair available does not mean you have to carry it as a sentence. It does mean the work of processing it looks different. You are not aiming for a clean fix that you can point to. You are aiming for the slow softening that comes when grief is met honestly and mercy is allowed to do its work over time.

For an unfixable regret, the practice tends to look like this. You let yourself feel the full weight of what you wish had been different, instead of trying to make it smaller. Speaking it out loud, to God or to a trusted person, is part of what keeps it from staying sealed inside you. And over time, you learn to remember that the person you are now is not the person who made that choice, and that growth is part of how God works on us. You hold the loss without trying to solve it, which is its own kind of faith.

This is slow work. It does not finish on a deadline. But it does soften. People who have walked this road will tell you that the regret never fully disappears, but it does stop running their life. There is a difference between carrying something and being crushed by it, and the difference is whether you have been able to lay it down, even a little, even just for tonight.

### Where Faith Meets Regret

Scripture does not treat regret as something to power past. The Bible is full of people who carried real regret and met God inside of it, not after they had cleaned themselves up.

Peter is the one I keep coming back to. He had spent three years with Jesus. He had been told that he would deny Him, and he had insisted he never would. Then he did, three times, in one night, while Jesus was being arrested and tried. After the rooster crowed, Peter went out and wept bitterly. That is not a person processing his feelings. That is a person broken open by what he has just learned about himself.

And here is what I want you to notice. Jesus does not write him off. After the resurrection, Jesus finds Peter on the beach. There is breakfast. There is a fire. And then, three times, mirroring the three denials, Jesus asks Peter if he loves Him. Three times, Peter says yes. Three times, Jesus tells him to feed His sheep.

That is restoration. Not pretending the denial did not happen, and not minimizing what Peter did. It is the kind of restoration that meets the failure honestly and still hands the person a future. The man who denied Christ on that night became the man who preached at Pentecost. The regret did not disqualify him. It became part of the texture of who he was as a leader of the early church, someone who knew exactly what it felt like to fall, and could meet other people who had fallen with mercy instead of judgment.

If God did that with Peter, what makes you think He cannot do it with you?

What changes things, biblically, is not the absence of regret. It is what regret gets handed over to.

Paul makes a distinction worth sitting with. He writes about a sorrow that produces repentance leading to salvation, and a sorrow of the world that produces death (2 Corinthians 7:10, NASB 2020). The same emotion can lead somewhere life-giving or somewhere corrosive, depending on what we do with it. Worldly sorrow is regret that loops endlessly without ever turning anywhere. Godly sorrow is regret that turns toward God and toward whatever repair or new way of walking is still possible.

Lamentations 3 is one of the most honest passages in Scripture about sitting in the middle of regret and grief. The writer holds nothing back about how dark it is. And then, in the middle of the lament, comes this: "The Lord's acts of mercy indeed do not end, for His compassions do not fail. They are new every morning; great is Your faithfulness" (Lamentations 3:22-23, NASB 2020). New every morning is not a promise that the past did not happen. It is a promise that mercy is not used up by yesterday.

That changes how you carry it.

### A Few Verses Worth Returning To

Different kinds of regret call for different kinds of Scripture. When the regret is mostly about who you used to be, Isaiah 43:18-19 speaks of God doing something new and not remembering former things. For regret tied to a person you hurt, 1 John 1:9 walks through confession and faithful forgiveness. Psalm 32 is a good place to sit when the regret is about a path you did not take, because it offers a picture of the rest that comes after honesty with God. And for the regret that aches over time you cannot get back, Joel 2:25 holds a promise about God restoring years that were lost.

Read them slowly, one at a time, not as a religious exercise but as words spoken to you. Let them have room to land.

### When to Reach for More Support

There is a difference between regret that comes and goes, and regret that has started running your life. If the loop has lasted for weeks or months and interrupts your sleep regularly, that is no longer just an emotional challenge to work through on your own. The same is true if regret has begun isolating you from people you love, or if it is carrying you toward hopelessness about your future.

Sometimes regret is tangled up with depression or with unresolved grief. Sometimes it is part of what counselors call moral injury, the kind of inner wound that forms when we believe we have violated our own conscience. All of these respond well to qualified care. A trained counselor or therapist can help you see what you cannot see from inside the loop. A wise pastor or trusted physician can be part of the same care. None of this is a failure of faith. It is part of how healing actually tends to work, and there is no shame in asking for it.

You do not have to carry this alone. You were never expected to.

### Closing

If you are sitting with something that has played on a loop for a long time, here is what I want you to hear. The fact that you still care about what happened says something true about you. Your conscience is alive. Love is involved in this somewhere, or you would not still be turning it over. That is not the voice of failure. That is the voice of a person who has not given up on becoming someone who does better next time.

If your regret is one that cannot be repaired, please remember Peter on the beach. The future Jesus handed him was not in spite of the failure. It came after the failure had been met honestly and forgiven, and folded into something new. That offer is still open. It has not closed because of what you did or did not do. Mercy is new every morning, including the one you are about to wake up to.

That person you have been so hard on deserves the same mercy she would offer a friend in her exact situation.
